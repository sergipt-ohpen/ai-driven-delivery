# 🏛️ Architecture Decisions, Explained for Non-Engineers

Engineering teams produce two artifacts that most PMs politely ignore: **ADRs** and **architecture diagrams**. That's a missed opportunity. You don't need to write them — but learning to *read* them (with Claude's help) gives you early warning on timelines, risk, and product constraints that otherwise surface as surprises mid-quarter.

## Why these documents historically didn't exist (and why they do now)

Architecture documentation has always been the first to go stale and the most expensive to be missing. The kickoff diagrams in PowerPoint are obsolete by the first refactor. The decisions nobody writes down become Slack conversations nobody can find two years later.

The cause was never laziness — it was **friction**. Maintaining a diagram in a drawing tool has too much overhead to do as part of everyday work; writing a decision record from scratch takes 30–45 minutes that "aren't in the ticket."

AI changed the economics. Diagrams are now written as plain text that lives next to the code (and renders as visuals automatically in GitHub, GitLab, and Notion), and decision records are **generated by AI from the discussion the team already had** — a Slack thread or a pull-request debate pasted into Claude comes out the other side as a structured record. When the cost of documenting drops to near zero, "we don't have time to document decisions" stops being a valid answer. That's worth knowing when you ask for it.

## What an ADR is, in plain terms

An **ADR — Architecture Decision Record** — is a short document that captures one significant technical decision: what was decided, what the alternatives were, and *why* the team chose this path. Think of it as the engineering equivalent of a product decision log.

Not every decision needs one. The pragmatic test engineers use: *if a new team member saw this today, would they ask "why on earth did they do it this way?"* If yes, there's an ADR missing. (The same test works remarkably well for product decisions.)

The dominant format today (called MADR) answers five questions:

- **Status** — is this still in force, accepted, or superseded by a later decision?
- **Context** — what situation forced a decision?
- **Options considered** — what alternatives were on the table?
- **Decision** — what did we choose, and why?
- **Consequences** — what does this make easier, and what does it make harder?

The **options considered** section is the one to read first as a PM: it tells you what was *rejected* and why, which is exactly what you need when a stakeholder asks "why don't we just use X?" — someone probably already evaluated X, and the answer is written down.

Here's what one looks like, translated into plain terms. A real ADR titled "Use a lightweight embedded database instead of a full database server" might say: *Context* — small team, predictable workload. *Options* — lightweight embedded DB, full server in containers, a managed cloud service. *Decision* — the lightweight one: zero infrastructure overhead, first-class support in our framework, the load doesn't justify more. *Consequences* — some advanced data types unavailable; migrating to a bigger database later means extra work; fine at our current scale. Every line of that is product-relevant: it encodes a scale assumption ("fine at our current scale") that becomes your problem the quarter growth exceeds it.

One convention matters more than it looks: ADRs are never deleted or rewritten — when a decision changes, a new ADR marks the old one as *superseded*. The result is a history of **why the system is the way it is**, which is exactly the question a PM asks two years later when a "simple" feature turns out to be expensive.

## What C4 diagrams are, in plain terms

**C4** is a convention (created by Simon Brown) for drawing software systems at four zoom levels, like maps:

1. **Context** — the whole system as one box, plus the users and external services it talks to. *This is the level made for you.*
2. **Containers** — the major running pieces: the web app, the mobile app, the API, the database. Still very readable for a PM.
3. **Components** — the internals of each piece. Engineering territory.
4. **Code** — class-level detail. Skip it.

The insight behind C4 is that most architecture arguments happen because people are talking at different zoom levels. As a PM, levels 1 and 2 tell you nearly everything you need: what the moving parts are, what depends on what, and where an external vendor sits in your critical path.

Two things about how modern teams produce these diagrams change what you can expect:

- **Diagrams are text.** Lightweight ones are written in Mermaid (a text notation GitHub and Notion render as visuals); more formal setups use Structurizr, where the team defines the architecture model *once* and every zoom level is generated from it — move a piece, and all the views update consistently. Text means version-controlled, reviewable, and readable by Claude (and by you).
- **Diagrams can be generated from the actual code.** Instead of someone drawing what they *believe* the system looks like, the AI reads the codebase and draws what it *actually* looks like. That kills the classic failure mode of architecture diagrams: confident, beautiful, and wrong.

## Why a PM should read these (not write them)

Every ADR is a trade-off, and trade-offs are product decisions wearing technical clothes:

- **"We chose the simpler option; it won't scale past X users"** — that's a roadmap constraint with a date on it.
- **"We're depending on vendor Y for payments"** — that's a risk register entry and a negotiation lever.
- **"This makes feature A easy and feature B hard"** — that's information you want *before* you commit B to the roadmap.

ADRs also explain the estimates you find puzzling. When "add a second currency" comes back as an eight-week job, there's usually a three-year-old ADR that explains exactly why. Reading it beats relitigating it.

Your role isn't to challenge the technical choice — it's to make sure the *product consequences* of the choice are visible, priced into the plan, and revisited when the assumptions behind them change.

## Using Claude as your translator

This is where AI removes the literacy barrier. ADRs and diagrams are text; Claude reads text. Practical moves:

- **Paste an ADR and ask for the product implications.** What does this decision make cheap, expensive, or impossible? What assumptions does it rest on, and what would invalidate them?
- **Paste a C4 context or container diagram and ask for a guided tour.** Which parts are ours vs. third-party? Where's the single point of failure? What breaks if vendor X has an outage?
- **Ask for a risk-and-timeline reading.** Given this set of decisions, which upcoming roadmap items are likely to collide with them?
- **Turn a product decision into a record.** The same trick engineers use works for you: paste the Slack thread where you decided to kill feature X into Claude and ask for a decision record — context, options considered, decision, consequences. Your future self will thank you.

One caution: Claude can translate what a document *says*, and reason about likely consequences — but the document may itself be outdated. Check the status field, check the date, and when a conclusion would change your roadmap, confirm it with the engineers who own the decision. Claude gets you to a good question fast; the answer still deserves a human check.

## The habit worth installing

Ask engineering where the ADRs live (if the answer is "we don't have any," that's worth a conversation in itself — with AI generating the first draft from a pasted discussion, the cost objection is gone, and the *why* of today's decisions is walking out the door with every departure). Then make it routine: when a new ADR lands, spend five minutes with Claude translating it into product terms. It's the cheapest risk-management practice available to you.

## 🛠️ Try it with Claude

```
Here is an Architecture Decision Record from my engineering team: [paste ADR]. Explain it to me as a non-technical product manager: (1) what was decided and why, in plain language, (2) which options were rejected and what that tells me, (3) what this makes easier or harder for the product going forward, (4) what assumptions it rests on and what would invalidate them, (5) three good questions I could ask the tech lead about it.
```

```
Here is a C4 container diagram of our system (as Mermaid text): [paste diagram]. Give me a guided tour for a PM: what each box does in one sentence, which parts are third-party dependencies, where the riskiest single point of failure is, and which parts would be involved if we built [describe upcoming feature].
```

```
Here is the discussion where we made a significant product decision: [paste Slack thread, meeting notes, or email chain]. Turn it into a decision record with this structure: Status, Context, Options considered (with the pros/cons that were actually raised), Decision, Consequences. Flag anything important that the discussion left ambiguous, so I can resolve it before this becomes the official record.
```
