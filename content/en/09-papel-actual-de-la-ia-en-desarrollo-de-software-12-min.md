## The current role of AI in software development

> In February 2026, Andrej Karpathy — one of the founders of OpenAI — declared that "vibe coding" was already out of fashion and proposed a different term for 2026: **agentic engineering**. The phrase is timely: in 18 months we went from "AI suggests lines to me" to "AI executes tasks for me for 200 minutes straight". This pre-course starts from there.

The bottleneck is moving: it's no longer **writing code**, but **defining what to build, providing effective context, and validating that what gets generated is useful**.

## What the hard data says (2025–2026)

### Near-universal adoption

-   **Stack Overflow Developer Survey 2025** (49,000 responses, 177 countries): **84%** of developers use or plan to use AI tools (vs. 76% in 2024). **51%** use them every day.
    
-   **DORA 2025** (Google Cloud, ~5,000 professionals): **90%** use AI at work (+14 points vs. 2024).
    
-   **GitHub Octoverse 2025**: 180 million devs on GitHub (+36M in one year, ≈1 new dev/second). **80%** of new developers use Copilot in their first week.
    

### A curious fact from Octoverse 2025

For the first time in more than a decade, **TypeScript overtook Python and JavaScript** as the most used language on GitHub (August 2025). GitHub attributes this to the *"convenience loop"* with AI: typed languages generate more useful guardrails for LLMs, which improves the code generation experience, which increases their usage, which feeds the cycle back.

> **About the programming language:** The live demos and exercises in the program will be developed primarily in JavaScript and TypeScript. However, it is not a requirement to know them beforehand nor to adopt them mandatorily. The concepts of agentic engineering, AI tool usage, and workflows you will learn apply to any technology stack. You can complete the exercises and the final project in the language you already master.

### But trust is falling

-   Stack Overflow 2025: trust in AI answers fell from **40% (2024) to 29% (2025)**. **46%** actively distrust them.
    
-   Only **3%** of developers report "high trust" in AI output.
    
-   **75%** still prefer asking a human when they doubt the AI's answer.
    

## The productivity-perception paradox

Here is the data point that should most challenge your assumptions before starting the master's program:

> **METR study (July 2025)**, RCT with 16 experienced developers on mature open source repositories (≥22k stars, ≥1M LOC), across 246 real tasks with Cursor Pro + Claude 3.5/3.7 Sonnet:
> 
> -   The devs **predicted** they would be **24% faster** with AI.
>     
> -   **After** the experiment, they believed they had been **20% faster**.
>     
> -   **In reality they were 19% slower**.
>

### How to read this study without drawing the wrong conclusion

The "19% slower" is **a solid data point within its sample and specific moment** (early 2025, huge and mature codebases), not a universal law. METR itself published (February 2026) that it is redesigning the experiment due to selection biases. What matters for you is:

-   In **greenfield + simple tasks**, the gains are real and sometimes massive (Stanford reports up to +30–40% in these contexts).
    
-   In **legacy, complex codebases with implicit conventions**, AI introduces **validation overhead, rework, and context-switching**. And perception deceives.
    
-   **Measure > assume**. That's the first operating principle of the master's program.
    

### The other uncomfortable finding (Stanford, October 2025)

A paper on arXiv (2510.10165) analyzed what happened after the introduction of Copilot in teams:

-   **Core** devs (more experienced) review **+6.5% more code**.
    
-   But their production of original code falls **−19%**.
    

The technical debt generated by AI **shifts toward the seniors**, who spend more time on code review.

## Software 3.0 and agentic engineering (Karpathy)

In his talk on **June 17, 2025** at the AI Startup School, Andrej Karpathy formalized:

-   **Software 1.0**: classic code written by humans.
    
-   **Software 2.0**: neural networks (the weights are the "code").
    
-   **Software 3.0**: LLMs programmable in natural language, with prompts as code and the context window as memory.
    

📖 Transcript and analysis: [Latent Space — Andrej Karpathy on Software 3.0](https://www.latent.space/p/s3)

**Vibe coding** was coined by Karpathy on X on February 2, 2025: *going with the flow and writing software by conversing with the LLM, "forgetting that the code exists"*.

> 🚨 **February 2026 update**: Karpathy himself declared that "vibe coding is already out of fashion" and proposed **agentic engineering** to distinguish professional use with supervision, oversight, and rigor.
> 
> *"Agentic because by default you're no longer writing 99% of the code, you're orchestrating agents and supervising. Engineering because there's an art and a science that can be learned."*

That difference — between vibe coding (mood, demos) and agentic engineering (supervision, production) — is exactly the line this master's program crosses.

## What's happening with the money

To understand the urgency of the moment, the investment numbers speak for themselves (figures as of April 2026):

-   **Cursor (Anysphere)**: USD **2,000M ARR** in February 2026 — the fastest B2B company to reach USD 2B. Discussed valuation: USD 50–60B.
    
-   **Cognition (Devin + Windsurf)**: round under discussion at a USD **25,000M valuation** (vs. USD 10.2B in September 2025).
    
-   **Total AI coding tools market**: estimated between USD 7.4B and USD 12.8B in 2026, with projections of USD 26–30B toward 2030.
    
-   **GitHub Copilot**: **20 million total users** (July 2025), **4.7 million paying** (January 2026, +75% YoY), **90% of the Fortune 100** have adopted it.
    

> 💡 Don't memorize these figures. What matters is the magnitude: at no point in the history of software development has there been this level of investment and concentration in tools that change how the individual dev works. That makes it mandatory to have an informed opinion on how to use them.

## The elephant in the room: the employment data

-   **SignalFire State of Talent 2025**: Big Tech cut hiring of recent graduates by **−25%** in 2024 vs. 2023. Grads now represent only **7%** of Big Tech hiring (vs. ~15% pre-pandemic).
    
-   **Stanford Digital Economy Lab 2025**: employment of software developers aged **22–25 fell ~20%** since late 2022. That of the 35–49 age group rose **+9%** in the same period.
    
-   The same companies **raised hiring of devs with 2–5 years of experience by +27%**.
    

📺 Erik Brynjolfsson (Stanford) explains the democratization of Seniority: [The "Canary in the Coal Mine" effect](https://www.hbs.edu/managing-the-future-of-work/podcast/Pages/podcast-details.aspx?episode=9166436185&utm_source=chatgpt.com)

The "senior dev" role is not being replaced, it is being **redefined**. And this master's program is one of the ways to make that transition on purpose instead of suffering it.

## The actionable takeaways from this page

1.  **Don't expect automatic productivity gains.** Expecting 10–20% on average is realistic; greenfield can deliver much more, legacy can be negative. **Measure your specific case.**
    
2.  **AI shifts load toward you as a senior.** More code review, more validation, more architectural decisions. Adjust your definition of *Done*.
    
3.  **The bottleneck moves to problem framing and context engineering.** These will be the pillars of the first sessions of the master's program.
    
4.  **The market is concentrating fast.** You don't need to master 10 tools, you need to master **1–2** well and understand how they connect.
